# Encoding Patterns — Extended Content

> **⚠ EXTENSION NOTE.** This file is encoding pedagogy: how the abstract storyform translates into concrete story moments. The original term files contain *definitions* but no patterns or worked examples. Examples here are reconstructed from publicly available Dramatica analyses (e.g., narrativefirst.com Casablanca/Star Wars analyses) and Claude's training knowledge. Sourcing-wise: the *structural facts* about the cited films come from published Dramatica analyses; the *encoding pedagogy* (how to use this for one's own work) is general theory plus interpretation.

---

## The Encoding Discipline

Encoding is the bridge between the storyform (abstract structure) and storytelling (concrete presentation). The same storyform can be encoded a thousand ways. The same story-moment can encode different storyforms. The discipline is:

1. **Locate the storyform position**: which Throughline, Class, Type, Variation, Element is this moment activating?
2. **Hold the *role*, vary the *clothes***: the storyform position is the role; the encoding is the costume. Change costumes freely, never change the role you've committed to.
3. **Use the Quad for complication-menu**: the four Elements of the active Quad give you ready-made variation/escalation paths.
4. **Verify with the Dynamic Pair**: every Element implies its opposite. If your encoding doesn't make the opposite *visible* somewhere (in a contrasting character, a missing presence, a counter-temptation), you're under-encoding.

## Worked Example 1 — *Casablanca* (1942)

A canonical Change-MC story. Public Dramatica analyses place its storyform roughly as:

| Slot | Choice | What it looks like in the film |
|------|--------|-------------------------------|
| OS Class | Universe | The fixed, war-trapped situation of Casablanca itself — refugees stuck, unable to leave |
| MC (Rick) Class | Mind | Rick's fixed attitude — cynicism, "I stick my neck out for nobody" |
| IC (Ilsa) Class | Universe (different Type than OS) | She *is* a fixed situation in Rick's life — the past returning |
| RS Class | Psychology | The renegotiation of who they are to each other (manipulation, longing) |
| MC Resolve | Change | Rick abandons cynicism for commitment |
| MC Growth | Start | Rick must *start* caring about the war |
| MC Approach | Be-er | Rick is reactive, responds to external events; he doesn't initiate |
| Driver | Decision | Decisions provoke actions throughout |
| Outcome | Success (the Goal — getting Laszlo and Ilsa out — is achieved) |
| Judgment | Good (Rick is at peace with his choice) |

### Encoding readouts

**MC Problem at the start of Act 1**: Rick's fixed attitude (Mind class). It's encoded as the cynical line "I stick my neck out for nobody," delivered repeatedly across early scenes. Same storyform-position, same role, multiple costumes (different scenes, different interlocutors).

**The IC's pressure**: Ilsa is the Impact Character — she carries the Universe-Class alternative (your past is what defines you, not what you decide today). She doesn't argue this in dialogue; she encodes it by *being there*, by her history with Rick being inescapably present.

**The Resolve moment**: the airport scene. Rick's Change is encoded by him *making the choice he wouldn't have made in Act 1* — sending Ilsa with Laszlo, joining the war effort. Same storyform-position (Change Resolve), encoded as a single action: handing over the letters of transit.

**Why the Change reads as Good Judgment**: Rick's fixed attitude was a *defense* against a wound, not a true position. By Changing, he reclaims a self he had buried. The film argues: *holding to that cynicism would have been wrong here*. Different stories make Change look bad (Faustian); this one makes it look like reclamation.

### What you can borrow from this for your own work

- **Costume freedom.** You could re-encode this exact storyform as: a war-trapped programmer in modern Berlin who's sworn off relationships after a betrayal, his ex returns with a need that requires him to use his connections. Same storyform, completely different storytelling.
- **The "fixed attitude vs. fixed situation" double** is what makes Casablanca's MC/IC pairing land. Both Rick (Mind) and Ilsa-as-IC (Universe) come from State classes — they're in *opposed* fixed-position Classes. The friction is that of two stuck things refusing to move until one of them does.

## Worked Example 2 — *Star Wars: A New Hope* (1977)

A canonical Change-MC quest with completely different Class arrangement.

| Slot | Choice | What it looks like in the film |
|------|--------|-------------------------------|
| OS Class | Physics | The rebellion as activity — battles, escapes, the Death Star plan |
| MC (Luke) Class | Universe | Luke's fixed situation — farm boy on Tatooine, orphan, uncle dead |
| IC (Obi-Wan) Class | Mind | Obi-Wan's fixed attitude — the Jedi worldview, "trust the Force" |
| RS Class | Psychology | The mentorship-as-manipulation: Obi-Wan teaching Luke to see differently |
| MC Resolve | Change | Luke abandons "let someone else do it" for active heroism |
| MC Growth | Start | Luke must start trusting his instincts / the Force |
| Driver | Action | Actions provoke decisions (the message → R2's escape → the homestead burning → leaving) |
| Outcome | Success (Death Star destroyed) |
| Judgment | Good (Luke at peace with his transformation) |

### Encoding readouts

**Same Resolve choice (Change), entirely different Class assignment than Casablanca.** Both films argue "Change is good" — but Casablanca encodes the Change as moral re-engagement, Star Wars encodes it as identity-claiming. The storyform-level question is the same; the storytelling is incommensurable.

**The "trench run" climax encodes the Change Resolve as a single act**: Luke turning off his targeting computer is the moment he commits to the IC's worldview ("trust the Force") and abandons his own ("rely on instruments"). One image, one choice, the entire MC arc collapsed into a beat.

**Why the IC has to be in a different Class from MC**: if Luke and Obi-Wan were both in Universe (both fixed situations), there'd be no genuine alternative being offered. Obi-Wan's *fixed attitude* (Mind) is the alternative to Luke's *fixed situation* (Universe) — internal vs. external state.

## Common Encoding Patterns by Slot

### MC Class = Universe (fixed situation)

The MC has a role, a status, a place that defines them and won't let them go. Encoding cues:
- They're known by what they *are*, not what they *do* (the orphan, the widow, the king, the slave)
- Early scenes show them embedded in the situation
- The IC challenges by offering a different *position* (literal or metaphorical)

### MC Class = Mind (fixed attitude)

The MC has a worldview, a stance, an emotional position they hold against the world. Encoding cues:
- They have a refrain — a thing they keep saying that signals their position
- Their stance is read as personality but is functionally a defense
- The IC challenges by *being* the alternative — embodying it rather than arguing it

### MC Class = Physics (locked-in activity)

The MC is in motion, doing — but doing the wrong thing or doing it wrong. Encoding cues:
- They're defined by their work, their role, their methodology
- Trying harder makes things worse
- The IC offers a different *kind* of doing

### MC Class = Psychology (locked-in pattern of thinking)

The MC has a habit-of-mind, a way they process problems. Encoding cues:
- They reach the wrong conclusions reliably (they're not stupid; they're patterned)
- Information doesn't help — they re-pattern it back to their preset
- The IC offers a different way of *processing*, not different conclusions

### Steadfast vs. Change as encoded payoff

- Steadfast climax: a moment when they *could* have changed but consciously didn't. Often quiet. The drama is the not-budging in the face of pressure that should have moved them.
- Change climax: a single action or word that reverses their established pattern. Often public, often costly.

## Encoding heuristics for non-narrative work

Dramatica's machinery transfers usefully into:

### Songs / lyrics (Suno work)

- A track typically encodes **one Element-pair** (one Quad-axis), not a whole storyform.
- Verse encodes one side of the Pair, refrain encodes the tension or the other side.
- Bridge can step out to the *companion* or *dependent* Element of the same Quad — not the dynamic-pair, that's the refrain's job.
- A Concept Album (e.g., a triptych like The Agency System) encodes a multi-Throughline storyform across tracks; individual tracks are scenes, the album is the throughline.

### Long-form narrative (novel-architect work)

- Each chapter typically inhabits one Type within one Throughline.
- A scene typically activates one Variation; a beat typically activates one Element.
- The chapter's *companion-pair Element* is what makes the scene feel rich rather than thin: even if the scene's Problem is "Test", the presence of "Cause" or "Effect" in the same scene makes it texturally complete.

### Conceptual structuring (spec-skill work, design docs)

- Dramatica's Class structure (External/Internal × State/Process) maps onto many design problems: a system's external-static-properties vs. internal-static-state vs. external-dynamic-behavior vs. internal-dynamic-process.
- The "Throughline" concept maps onto multi-stakeholder analyses: each stakeholder's perspective can be modeled as a Throughline with its own Concern, Issue, Problem.

## Anti-patterns to watch for

1. **Encoding without locating.** "It's a story about freedom" — without locating freedom on the storyform (is it a Concern? an Issue? a Solution Element?), you can't tell whether your scenes are advancing the form or drifting.

2. **Costume change masquerading as structure change.** Re-encoding doesn't shift the storyform. If you re-encode a scene from "argument in a kitchen" to "argument in a spaceship", that's free; if you change Resolve from Steadfast to Change, that's a different story.

3. **Single-Element encoding.** A moment that only activates one Element feels thin. Quad-encoding (touching three of the four corners) feels rich.

4. **Throughline confusion.** Mistaking a scene's *Throughline* (whose perspective is this?) is the most common error. A scene can be "about" the OS Goal but written from the IC's perspective — that's an IC-throughline scene with OS subject matter, not an OS scene.

5. **Treating Dramatica as prescription rather than diagnostic.** Dramatica describes complete-story structure. It doesn't say *write this way*; it says *if your story is complete, this is what it looks like*. Use it diagnostically.
