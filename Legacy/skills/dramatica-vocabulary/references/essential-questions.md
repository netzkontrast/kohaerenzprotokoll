# Essential Questions — Storyform Workflow

> **⚠ EXTENSION NOTE.** The "Essential Questions" framing is from Phillips & Huntley's published Dramatica documentation; the specific list and ordering presented here is a synthesis of public sources (storymind.com, *Dramatica: A New Theory of Story*) and Claude's training knowledge. Different sources present slightly different orderings; this one is structured for practical workflow rather than theoretical purity.

---

## Why these questions

Dramatica's full storyform is set by ~12 high-leverage choices. Make these 12 deliberately and the engine derives the rest of the storyform automatically. This file walks them in workflow order: choices that constrain other choices come first.

When you don't know an answer, **don't guess** — leave it open and come back. The downstream constraints often reveal the upstream choice you didn't realize you were making.

---

## Phase 1 — Throughline Class assignments

### Q1. Where does the Overall Story problem live?

Pick one Class for the OS Throughline: Universe, Physics, Psychology, or Mind. Ask:
- Is the shared problem an **external situation** that traps everyone? → Universe
- Is the shared problem an **external activity** that's going wrong? → Physics
- Is the shared problem a **way of thinking** that's malfunctioning? → Psychology
- Is the shared problem a **fixed attitude** that nobody can release? → Mind

Examples: a heist (Physics), a war zone (Universe), a cult (Psychology), a grieving family (Mind).

### Q2. Where does the Main Character's personal problem live?

Pick the MC's Class — must be **diametrically opposed** to the IC's Class (decided next), so think of these two together.

The MC↔IC pair is always one of:
- Universe ↔ Mind (state-pair)
- Physics ↔ Psychology (process-pair)

Ask: Is the MC's personal struggle **what they're stuck in** (situation, attitude — State) or **what they're stuck doing** (activity, mental pattern — Process)?

### Q3. Where does the Impact Character live?

Whichever pole of the diagonal the MC is *not* on. The IC must offer the genuine alternative the MC almost-but-doesn't take (Steadfast) or eventually does take (Change).

After Q1–Q3, the Relationship Story Class is determined: it's whichever Class is left. (OS + MC + IC + RS = all four Classes, no overlap.)

---

## Phase 2 — Within each Throughline: Concern, Issue, Problem

### Q4. What is the OS Concern (Type)?

Within the OS Class, pick which of the four Types is the focal area. The Concern is *not* the goal-statement; it's the area where the goal lives.

- OS in Universe: Past / Present / Future / Progress
- OS in Physics: Learning / Understanding / Doing / Obtaining
- OS in Psychology: Conceiving / Conceptualizing / Being / Becoming
- OS in Mind: Memory / Preconscious / Conscious / Subconscious

The OS Concern often *is* the genre-frame: a Physics-Obtaining story is a heist or quest; a Mind-Conscious story is a pondering film.

### Q5. What is the MC Concern, IC Concern, RS Concern?

Each Throughline has its own Concern within its own Class. The four Concerns together carry the four KTAD perspectives on the underlying problem.

### Q6. What is each Throughline's Issue (Variation)?

Within each Concern, pick the focal Variation. **The Issue is where Theme lives.** If you've identified your story's theme — even loosely — the Issue is its formal home.

Theme is typically explored as the Issue *in dynamic-pair tension with its opposite Variation in the same Quad.* Example: theme of "merit vs. inheritance" might be Issue=`Worth` (in tension with `Value`) under Mind/Conscious.

### Q7. What is each Throughline's Problem (Element)?

Each Throughline has a Problem Element — the source-of-conflict at the most granular level. The Solution Element is automatically the dynamic-pair-opposite of the Problem.

This is the deepest commitment in the storyform. Once you set Problem Elements, most of the rest of the storyform is constrained.

## Phase 3 — Main Character Dynamics

### Q8. Resolve: Steadfast or Change?

At the climax, does the MC abandon their original approach (Change) or hold to it (Steadfast)? Ask: *what does my story's argument prove* — that the MC was right to hold on, or right to let go?

This is the single highest-leverage choice for the MC arc. Misidentifying Resolve makes the entire storyform feel wrong.

### Q9. Growth: Stop or Start?

Is the MC's arc about **stopping** something (a doubt, a habit, a self-defeat) or **starting** something (a commitment, a connection, a self-possession)?

Steadfast often pairs with Stop, Change often pairs with Start — but these are tendencies, not rules. Steadfast+Start and Change+Stop are valid and produce specific story-flavors.

### Q10. Approach: Do-er or Be-er?

Is the MC's first move when faced with a problem to **act on the world** (Do-er) or to **adjust themselves** (Be-er)?

Do-ers reach for tools; Be-ers reach for stillness. This affects every decision-point in encoding.

### Q11. Mental Sex (Problem-Solving Style): Linear or Holistic?

Does the MC solve via **sequential cause-effect logic** (Linear) or via **balancing-the-field relational adjustment** (Holistic)?

Independent of biological sex/gender. This affects how the MC notices and processes problems, not whether they care.

## Phase 4 — Plot Dynamics

### Q12. Driver: Action or Decision?

Are the story's beats provoked by **actions** that force decisions (Action-driven), or by **decisions** that force actions (Decision-driven)?

Test: pick three major turning points. Was each one an action that triggered a decision-response, or a decision that triggered an action-response? They should consistently match the Driver setting.

### Q13. Limit: Optionlock or Timelock?

Does the climax come because **alternatives run out** (Optionlock) or because **time runs out** (Timelock)?

Optionlock examples: a whodunit (you've ruled out everyone but X), a relationship film (you've tried everything else). Timelock examples: ticking-bomb thrillers, deadline films, "before sunset" romances.

### Q14. Outcome: Success or Failure?

Does the OS Goal get achieved, or not?

Note: this is the *Story* Goal (OS Concern) — not necessarily the MC's personal goal. The MC can fail personally while the OS Goal succeeds, or vice versa.

### Q15. Judgment: Good or Bad?

Is the MC at peace at the end, or unfulfilled?

Judgment is the MC's emotional standing; Outcome is the story-level achievement. The four combinations of Outcome × Judgment produce four distinct story endings (see `dramatica-fundamentals.md` → Resolve table).

## What gets derived automatically

After these ~14 choices, the engine determines:

- The Solution Element for each Throughline (dynamic-pair-opposite of the Problem)
- The Symptom and Response Elements (the misdirection — what the MC and others *think* the problem is)
- The Focus Element
- The Type-sequence (Acts) for each Throughline
- All Variation and Element placements at every level
- Character Element distributions across the 8 archetypal slots

This is the cascade. You don't have to decide all this — you decide the high-leverage ~14 choices and the engine fills in the rest consistently.

---

## When you don't know — leave it open

Not every choice is decidable up-front. Common sequence problems:

- **You can't decide Resolve until you know the Outcome and Judgment** — and sometimes vice versa. If you're stuck on Q8, jump to Q14–Q15 and see if the climax shape suggests it.
- **You can't decide Class until you know the Goal** — and sometimes vice versa. If Q1 is uncertain, sketch a few candidate Goals and see which one feels right; that often reveals the Class.
- **You can't decide MC Concern until you've lived with the MC for a while.** Drafts are also storyform-discovery tools.

The storyform isn't an outline you fill in; it's a constraint-satisfaction problem you converge on. Leave gaps, work the constraints, return.

## Worked walkthrough: applying this to *Casablanca*

| Q | Choice | Reasoning |
|---|--------|-----------|
| Q1 | OS = Universe | The fixed war-trapped situation of the city |
| Q2 | MC = Mind | Rick's fixed attitude (cynicism) |
| Q3 | IC = Universe | Ilsa as the past-as-fixed-situation |
| (RS by elimination = Psychology) | | |
| Q4 | OS Concern = Future | Will refugees escape? |
| Q5–Q7 | (deeper, requires more analysis) | |
| Q8 | Resolve = Change | Rick reverses his cynicism |
| Q9 | Growth = Start | Rick must start caring again |
| Q10 | Approach = Be-er | Rick reacts; he doesn't initiate |
| Q11 | Mental Sex = ? | Less clear; arguable Linear |
| Q12 | Driver = Decision | Decisions provoke actions throughout |
| Q13 | Limit = Optionlock | Climax when options exhaust, not deadline |
| Q14 | Outcome = Success | Laszlo+Ilsa escape, the Goal achieved |
| Q15 | Judgment = Good | Rick at peace with his choice |

Even with Q5–Q7 left open, the rest of the choices already constrain the storyform to a small handful of valid completions. The engine would derive the full structure from there.
