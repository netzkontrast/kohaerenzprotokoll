## §1 Class choice

Four Classes — pick **one** for OS; the other three are constrained by the
dynamic-pair rule (OS+SS pair; MC+IC pair; pairs are Universe↔Mind and
Physics↔Psychology).

| If the OS is fundamentally about… | Class |
|---|---|
| being trapped in a *situation* / status / circumstance that needs to change | **Universe** |
| *activities* / processes / missions / fights / journeys / builds / hunts | **Physics** |
| everyone's *frozen attitudes* — prejudice, grief, conviction, dogma | **Mind** |
| *schemes / manipulations / persuasions / mental shifts / psychological warfare* | **Psychology** |

**Test (verbs):** write the OS in one sentence. "The town *is in* a state of …" → Universe.
"The crew *hunts* …" → Physics. "The community *believes* …" → Mind.
"He *manipulates* her into …" → Psychology.

**MC's Class** is constrained to one of the two Classes *not* used by OS+SS.
Once OS is fixed, MC's options narrow to two.

## §2 Change vs Steadfast (MC Resolve)

Both are valid arcs. They argue *different things*.

**Change indicators**
- MC's defining trait/approach/belief at the start *is the problem* of their personal arc.
- The IC by their existence keeps showing the MC their approach isn't working.
- In the climax, the MC **abandons** their core driving Element and tries the opposite.
- Personal arc reads as growth / transformation / conversion.
- *Examples: Luke (faith over technology), Scout (acceptance over prejudice).*

**Steadfast indicators**
- MC's defining trait/approach/belief is *already correct* at the start; the *world* is what's wrong.
- The IC tries to convert the MC; the MC **resists**.
- In the climax, the MC **holds to** their core Element despite all pressure.
- Personal arc reads as endurance / conviction / redemption-of-others.
- *Examples: Atticus (Mockingbird), Job, Dr. Kimble (The Fugitive).*

**The test:** at the moment of maximum pressure at the climax, does the MC
*abandon* their core position or *hold* to it?

**Common confusion:** MC *Growth* is not MC *Change*. A Steadfast MC can
still grow — develop, mature, deepen *while holding* their core position.
Don't confuse "the MC develops" with "the MC switches".

## §3 Start vs Stop (MC Growth)

- **Start** — growing *into* something the MC lacks ("I need to learn to trust").
- **Stop** — growing *out of* something the MC has too much of ("I need to stop running").

Independent of Resolve. A Change-Start MC ends having gained a missing
trait; a Change-Stop MC ends having shed a corrosive one.

---

## §4 Do-er vs Be-er (MC Approach)

- **Do-er** — solves problems by *acting on the world* (build it, fight it, fix it).
- **Be-er** — solves problems by *adapting themselves* (rethink, reframe, endure).

**Test:** when the MC faces a problem and has 30 seconds to react, what's
their first move — *change the world*, or *change their stance toward it*?

**Anti-indicator:** "an introvert is a Be-er, an extrovert is a Do-er" —
unrelated. Approach is *problem-solving disposition*, not social style.

## §5 Linear vs Holistic (MC Mental Sex)

> **Label note.** The source book uses *Male / Female problem-solving*; the
> labels are dated and read as gender-essentialist. The cognitive distinction
> is real and gender-independent. Use **Linear / Holistic** in working notes;
> explain the original term once if speaking with collaborators.

**Linear indicators**
- MC solves problems by tracing cause-and-effect chains.
- "If A, then B, then C — therefore I'll do C." Sequential, single-thread.
- Faced with a problem, MC asks **"what's the next step?"**

**Holistic indicators**
- MC solves problems by attending to multiple relationships at once.
- "A and B and C are all connected; the imbalance is here, so I'll address the imbalance."
- Faced with a problem, MC asks **"what's out of balance?"**

**The test:** watch the MC in Act 1, first real problem they hit — do they
*step through it* (Linear) or *take in the whole pattern* (Holistic)?

**Anti-indicator:** "MC is logical, so must be Linear." Logic vs Feeling is
a *different* distinction (those are Motivation Elements). Linear/Holistic
is about reasoning **strategy**, not emotional vs rational.

## §6 Action Driver vs Decision Driver

The most-confused Plot Dynamic. Both involve actions and decisions; the
question is which **causes the other at major story transitions**.

**Action Driver** — an *action* happens; in response, *decisions* are
forced. Transitions are externally forced. *Star Wars: first the Empire
attacked Princess Leia's ship; then Luke had to decide whether to leave
Tatooine. First action, then decision.*

**Decision Driver** — a *decision* is made; in consequence, *actions*
unfold. Transitions are internally forced. *Casablanca: first Rick decided
to send Ilsa away; then he shot Strasser. First decision, then action.*

**The test that actually works:** for each major act transition, ask
*"what came first — the action or the decision?"* If actions come first
across the board → Action Driver. If decisions come first across the
board → Decision Driver. **Mix → your Driver isn't consistent yet;** pick
one and revise the other transition.

**Anti-indicator:** *"there's both action and decision throughout"* — yes,
every story has both. The dynamic isn't asking what's present; it's
asking what **triggers** what at transitions.

## §7 Optionlock vs Timelock (Story Limit)

**Optionlock** — story ends because *options run out*. Characters work
through possibilities; when the last fails (or succeeds), the story is over.
*Common in mysteries, heists, exhaustive searches. "We've tried everything."*

**Timelock** — story ends because *time runs out*. A clock is ticking; when
it hits zero, the outcome is locked.
*Common in thrillers, ticking-bomb plots, beat-the-deadline. "We have until midnight."*

**The test:** when you imagine the story's end, what makes the climax
*climactic*? *"This was the last possibility"* → Optionlock. *"We ran out
of time"* → Timelock.

**Anti-indicator:** *"both — running out of time **and** options"* — can
happen as flavor, but **at the structural level only one is the limit**.
Pick the one whose *failure* would actually end the story.

## §8 Outcome × Judgment

These are **independent**. Pick each on its own; read off the ending type.

|                       | Outcome: Success    | Outcome: Failure   |
|-----------------------|---------------------|--------------------|
| **Judgment: Good**    | **Triumph**         | **Personal Triumph** |
| **Judgment: Bad**     | **Personal Tragedy**| **Tragedy**          |

**Outcome decision:** does the **OS Goal** get achieved? *Narrowly about
the Goal* — not "do good things happen overall".

**Judgment decision:** does the **MC end in a satisfied internal state**?
Independent of whether the OS goal was achieved.
- *Personal Triumph: the world's problem isn't solved, but the MC is okay.*
- *Personal Tragedy: the world's problem is solved, but the MC paid for it personally.*

**The test that separates them:** imagine the final scene. Two questions:
1. Has the Goal been achieved? (Yes = Success / No = Failure)
2. Is the MC at peace internally? (Yes = Good / No = Bad)

If you keep collapsing them into "happy vs sad ending", you're not yet
using both dynamics — that's a flatter story than Dramatica is built to
model.

## §9 Goal level (Type, not Variation/Element)

The Goal **must be a Type** — one of the 16. The 4 Type-Quads per Class:

| Class | Four Types |
|---|---|
| Universe | Past, Progress, Present, Future |
| Physics | Understanding, Doing, Learning, Obtaining |
| Mind | Memory, Preconscious (Impulsive Responses), Conscious (Contemplation), Subconscious (Innermost Desires) |
| Psychology | Conceiving An Idea, Being (Playing A Role), Becoming (Changing One's Nature), Conceptualizing (Developing A Plan) |

**Common patterns:**
- *Obtaining* (Physics) — get the artifact, win the prize, earn the position.
- *Doing* (Physics) — accomplish the mission, perform the act.
- *Becoming* (Psychology) — transform the protagonist or antagonist into someone else.
- *Future* (Universe) — establish or prevent a future state.
- *Understanding* (Physics) — figure out what's actually going on.
- *Learning* (Physics) — acquire information that changes the situation.

**The test:** pitch your Goal at multiple levels and check granularity.
- ❌ Too coarse: *"the goal is winning"* — not a Type.
- ❌ Too fine: *"the goal is faith"* — Faith is an Element.
- ✅ Type level: *"Obtaining the Death Star plans"*, *"Becoming a Jedi"*,
  *"the Future of the Republic"*.

If your Goal sounds too vague to be a Type, you haven't picked one yet;
if it sounds too specific (a single Element), you've zoomed past Type.

## §10 Crucial Element coherence (Step 6 only)

The Crucial Element is **one of the 64 OS Character Elements**. The MC sits
on it; the IC sits on its dynamic-pair partner. Pair-lookup via
`tools/dramatica-nav/nav.py by-id <element-id> --include-pairs`.

Coherence rule (MC Resolve × Element role):

| MC Resolve | Element role | Storyform meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Change** | the **problem** | MC must *give it up* at the climax |
| **Steadfast** | the **solution** | MC must *hold to it* under pressure |

Any other combination → loop back to Step 3 (Resolve) or Step 6 (Element).

*Example: To Kill A Mockingbird — Crucial Element is INEQUITY. Scout
(MC) is Change → Inequity is the problem (her prejudice against Boo
Radley); Scout gives it up.*
