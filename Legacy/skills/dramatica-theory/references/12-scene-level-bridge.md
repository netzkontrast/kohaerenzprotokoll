# Scene-Level Bridge

> The storyform is abstract. The scene is concrete. This file is the bridge: how to take a storyform decision and let it inform what happens in the next scene you write — and conversely, how to audit a written scene against the storyform.

---

## The scene-audit workflow

For any single scene — drafted or planned — five questions, in order. Each one narrows what the scene is structurally doing.

### Q1: Which throughline is dominant?

A scene serves one of OS, MC, IC, SS *primarily*. It can serve more than one (most scenes do), but one is dominant — the throughline whose work this scene is most advancing.

Test: if you cut this scene, which throughline loses progress?

| Dominance | What the scene tends to feature |
|---|---|
| OS-dominant | Multiple characters, objective conflict, plot-moving events |
| MC-dominant | MC alone or in private, internal experience, MC's personal arc |
| IC-dominant | IC active, exposing the IC's nature/throughline, MC may not be present |
| SS-dominant | MC↔IC interaction *as a relationship*, not as combatants in OS |

### Q2: Which throughlines are also present?

A scene is rarely single-throughline. List the secondary ones too.

OS + MC is the most common combo (the MC acting within the objective story). MC + IC is common (the relationship in motion). All four in one scene = climax-level density.

### Q3: At what level is the scene operating?

For each throughline present, identify the level:

- **Class-level:** scene establishes or reinforces the throughline's domain (rare, mostly in opening scenes).
- **Type-level (Concern):** scene works the throughline's concern in broad strokes.
- **Variation-level (Issue):** scene explores the throughline's thematic question concretely.
- **Element-level (Problem):** scene digs into the specific motivation Element driving conflict.

The levels stack. A late-act scene often operates simultaneously at all four — Class is just the air, Type is the concern, Variation is the question being argued, Element is the specific tension on the table.

### Q4: Which static/progressive plot story point is the scene serving?

For OS scenes especially, ask which story point the scene advances:

- **Goal-pursuit** — characters move toward the OS Goal.
- **Requirement-met** — a Requirement gets fulfilled (or fails).
- **Consequence-shown** — Consequences arrive or get foreshadowed.
- **Forewarning** — a sign that Consequences are coming.
- **Dividend-delivered** — partial reward along the way.
- **Cost-paid** — partial loss along the way.
- **Prerequisite-established** — a precondition for Requirements gets set.
- **Precondition-imposed** — author-imposed extra condition shows up.

Plus the progressive ones: which **Signpost** of the throughline is this scene in? Which **Journey** between Signposts is the scene part of?

A scene that doesn't serve any plot story point may still be doing thematic or character work — but if it doesn't serve any of *those* either, it's probably cuttable.

### Q5: Which Motivation Elements are in play?

The 64 Motivation Elements are the deepest level. Most scenes activate 2–6 of them across the present characters. Identify which.

If your MC's Element is *Pursue* and the IC's is *Avoid*, scenes with both characters should activate that pair. If they don't, the scene isn't doing the MC↔IC structural work.

## Throughline-dominance patterns by act

Rough frequencies — not rules. Use them as priors when planning a scene mix.

| Act | OS-dominant | MC-dominant | IC-dominant | SS-dominant |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Act 1 (setup) | High — establish OS conflict | Medium — establish MC | Low — IC enters | Low — relationship begins |
| Act 2 (development) | Medium — OS develops | High — MC's arc deepens | Medium — IC challenges | Medium — relationship strains |
| Act 3 (escalation) | High — OS pressure peaks | Medium — MC at crisis | High — IC peaks too | High — relationship at breaking point |
| Act 4 (climax + resolution) | High — Goal pursuit climaxes | High — MC Resolve enacted | Medium — IC's effect lands | High — relationship resolves |

If your draft is heavy on OS-dominant scenes throughout and light on MC/SS, the audience will feel events but not arc. If it's heavy on MC-dominant and light on OS, the audience will feel arc but not stakes.

## Scene types by storyform function

These are the recurring scene shapes Dramatica's structure tends to call for. Not exhaustive — but if you find yourself stuck on what scene to write next, scanning this list is faster than reverse-engineering from the storyform.

### OS scenes

- **Goal-pursuit scene** — characters take action toward the Goal.
- **Requirement scene** — a Requirement is attempted; it succeeds or fails.
- **Forewarning scene** — early signs that Consequences are arriving become visible.
- **Cost scene** — characters pay something to keep pursuing the Goal.
- **Dividend scene** — characters receive a partial reward.
- **Antagonist-pressure scene** — Antagonist actively prevents/reconsiders.

### MC scenes

- **MC-alone scene** — MC's interior, private experience, often pivotal for Resolve.
- **MC-with-Crucial-Element scene** — the specific Element driving MC's arc gets foregrounded.
- **MC-decision scene** — MC chooses something that reflects (or contradicts) their core position.
- **MC-and-throughline-Class scene** — MC interacts with their Class-domain (the situation, the activity, the fixed attitude, the manipulation that defines their personal struggle).

### IC scenes

- **IC-introduction scene** — IC's nature gets established for the audience.
- **IC-challenges-MC scene** — IC by their existence calls MC's worldview into question.
- **IC-alone scene** — rare but powerful when used; lets IC's throughline land independent of MC.

### SS scenes

- **Relationship-building scene** — the MC↔IC relationship deepens or strains as its own thing.
- **Relationship-test scene** — the relationship faces a pressure that tests its core.
- **Relationship-pivot scene** — the relationship transforms (or fails to).

## When the storyform is silent on a scene

Sometimes you're stuck on what the next scene should be and the storyform doesn't obviously dictate one. Three useful prompts:

1. **What's the throughline most behind on?** Look at Act-X-so-far and count scene-shares per throughline. The under-served throughline probably wants a scene next.
2. **What Signpost is each throughline currently at?** If a throughline is overdue for the next Signpost, the next scene is probably its Journey-or-Signpost transition.
3. **What story point hasn't been touched yet this act?** Any of the eight static/passenger plot story points without a scene in this act probably needs one before the act ends.

If none of those generate a scene idea, the storyform may not be the limiting factor — the encoding/voice/subject-matter level might be where you're stuck. Switch frameworks for that question.

## Scene-revision use

For an existing scene that feels off:

1. Run the five-question audit on it.
2. If Q1 is unclear (no dominant throughline), the scene is probably trying to do too much — split it or focus it.
3. If Q4 is empty (no plot story point served), the scene may be character-only, fine, but check that it's serving a Concern/Issue/Problem at least.
4. If Q5 is empty (no Motivation Elements active), the scene has no mechanical conflict-driver. Either find which Elements *should* be in play given who's present, or accept the scene as transitional and keep it brief.
