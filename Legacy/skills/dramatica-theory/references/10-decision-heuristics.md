# Decision Heuristics

> The Dramatica decisions that authors actually struggle with. Each section gives indicators (signs pointing toward one option), anti-indicators (signs pointing away), and a short test. None of these are infallible — they're calibrated bets. When a heuristic and the story disagree, the story wins; the disagreement just means the heuristic doesn't apply to your case.

---

## Choosing the Class for each throughline

The four Classes:
- **Universe** (Situation): an external state. The conflict comes from being stuck in or oriented around a *circumstance*.
- **Physics** (Activity): an external process. The conflict comes from *doing* — actions, movements, operations.
- **Mind** (Fixed Attitude): an internal state. The conflict comes from a way of *thinking that won't shift*.
- **Psychology** (Manipulation): an internal process. The conflict comes from *thinking shifting* under pressure — schemes, persuasions, breakdowns.

### Class for the Overall Story

Ask: "Across all characters, what's the *nature* of the conflict?"

| If the OS is fundamentally about... | Class |
|---|---|
| ...being trapped in a situation, a place, a status, a circumstance that needs to change | Universe |
| ...activities, processes, missions, fights, journeys, builds, hunts | Physics |
| ...everyone's frozen attitudes — prejudice, grief, conviction, dogma | Mind |
| ...schemes, manipulations, persuasions, mental shifts, psychological warfare | Psychology |

Test: write the OS in one sentence. The verbs you reach for tell you the Class. "The town *is in* a state of …" → Universe. "The crew *hunts* …" → Physics. "The community *believes* …" → Mind. "He *manipulates* her into …" → Psychology.

### Class for the Main Character

Ask: "What's the MC's personal problem-domain?" — independent of the OS.

| If the MC's personal struggle is about... | Class |
|---|---|
| ...a personal situation they're stuck in (orphan, exile, trapped marriage, illness) | Universe |
| ...things they're personally doing or failing to do | Physics |
| ...their own fixed attitude (a grief they can't release, a belief they can't shake) | Mind |
| ...their own shifting/manipulating mind (self-deception, temptation, internal scheming) | Psychology |

The MC's Class is constrained: it's one of the two Classes *not* used by OS-SS. So once OS is fixed, MC's options narrow to two.

### Class for the Impact Character

The IC's Class is the dynamic-pair partner of the MC's. So once MC is fixed, IC is determined. But the question still has content: does the IC challenge the MC by *what they do*, *what their situation is*, *what they think*, or *how they manipulate*? Answer this to verify the Class assignment makes sense for the character you've already imagined.

### Class for the Subjective Story

The SS's Class is the dynamic-pair partner of the OS's. So once OS is fixed, SS is determined. But verify: is the MC↔IC relationship-as-its-own-story really *about* the kind of thing that Class implies?

If OS=Physics → SS=Psychology: the relationship is about psychological dynamics between MC and IC.
If OS=Mind → SS=Universe: the relationship is about the situation between them.

## Change vs Steadfast (MC Resolve)

These are not "good MC vs bad MC". Both are valid arcs. They argue different things.

### Change indicators

- The MC's defining trait/approach/belief at the start *is* the problem driving their personal arc.
- The IC by their existence keeps showing the MC that their approach isn't working.
- In the climax, the MC abandons their core driving Element and tries the opposite.
- Personal arc reads as growth/transformation/conversion.
- Examples: Luke (faith over technology), Scout (acceptance over prejudice).

### Steadfast indicators

- The MC's defining trait/approach/belief is *already correct* at the start; the world is what's wrong.
- The IC tries to convert the MC away from their approach; the MC resists.
- In the climax, the MC holds to their core Element despite all pressure.
- Personal arc reads as endurance/conviction/redemption-of-others.
- Examples: Atticus (Mockingbird), Job (in the book of Job), Dr. Kimble (The Fugitive).

### The test

Walk to the climax of your draft. In the moment of maximum pressure, does the MC *abandon* their core position or *hold* to it?

### Common confusion

MC Growth is not MC Change. A Steadfast MC can still grow — they can develop, mature, deepen *while holding* their core position. Atticus grows in *Mockingbird* but doesn't change. Don't confuse "the MC develops" with "the MC switches".

## Action Driver vs Decision Driver

This is the most-confused Plot Dynamic. Both involve actions and decisions; the question is which one *causes* the other at major story transitions.

### Action Driver

- An *action* happens. In response, *decisions* are forced.
- Story transitions are externally forced — something happens, characters must respond.
- Test: "First the Empire attacked Princess Leia's ship; then Luke had to decide whether to leave Tatooine." First action, then decision = Action Driver.
- Star Wars is Action Driver.

### Decision Driver

- A *decision* is made. In consequence, *actions* unfold.
- Story transitions are internally forced — characters decide, and the world rearranges.
- Test: "First Rick decided to send Ilsa away; then he shot Strasser." First decision, then action = Decision Driver.
- Casablanca is Decision Driver.

### The test that actually works

For each major act transition, ask: "What came *first* — the action or the decision?" If actions come first across the board, Action Driver. If decisions come first across the board, Decision Driver. If you get a mix — the inciting event is action but the second-act break is decision — your Story Driver isn't consistent yet, and you should pick one and revise the other transition.

### Anti-indicator

"There's both action and decision throughout the story" — yes, every story has both. The dynamic isn't asking what's present; it's asking what *triggers* what at the transitions.

## Linear vs Holistic (Mental Sex)

**Note on the label.** Phillips & Huntley call this "Male / Female problem-solving". The labels are dated and read as gender-essentialist; the cognitive distinction they describe is real and gender-independent. Use Linear / Holistic in working notes and explain the original term once when speaking with collaborators.

### Linear indicators

- MC solves problems by tracing cause-and-effect chains.
- "If A, then B, then C — therefore I'll do C."
- Sequential reasoning. Single thread of focus at a time.
- Faced with a problem, the MC asks "what's the next step?"

### Holistic indicators

- MC solves problems by attending to multiple relationships at once.
- "A and B and C are all connected; the imbalance is here, so I'll address the imbalance."
- Pattern-relational reasoning. Multiple threads held simultaneously.
- Faced with a problem, the MC asks "what's out of balance?"

### The test

Watch your MC in Act 1. The first time they hit a real problem, do they *step through it* (Linear) or do they *take in the whole pattern* (Holistic)? Their unmonitored cognitive habit is the answer.

### Anti-indicator

"My MC is logical, so they must be Linear." Logic vs Feeling is a different distinction (those are Motivation Elements). Linear/Holistic is about reasoning *strategy*, not about emotional vs rational.

## Choosing the Story Goal (at Type level)

The Goal must be a Type — one of the 16. Per the source book, the four Type Quads per Class are:

| Class | Four Types |
|---|---|
| Universe | Past, Progress, Present, Future |
| Physics | Understanding, Doing, Learning, Obtaining |
| Mind | Memory, Impulsive Responses (Preconscious), Contemplation (Conscious), Innermost Desires (Subconscious) |
| Psychology | Conceiving An Idea, Being (Playing A Role), Becoming (Changing One's Nature), Conceptualizing (Developing A Plan) |

The Goal is selected from the OS Class's four Types. Most commonly, Goals fall into one of these patterns:

- *Obtaining* (Physics) — get the artifact, win the prize, earn the position.
- *Doing* (Physics) — accomplish the mission, perform the act.
- *Becoming* (Psychology) — transform the protagonist or the antagonist into someone else.
- *Future* (Universe) — establish or prevent a future state.
- *Understanding* (Physics) — figure out what's actually going on.
- *Learning* (Physics) — acquire information that changes the situation.

### The test

Pitch your Goal at multiple levels and check the granularity:
- Wrong (too coarse): "the goal is winning" — that's not a Type.
- Wrong (too fine): "the goal is faith" — Faith is an Element, not a Type.
- Right (Type level): "the goal is *Obtaining* the Death Star plans" or "the goal is *Becoming* a Jedi" or "the goal is the *Future* of the Republic".

If your Goal sounds too vague to be a Type, you haven't picked one yet; if it sounds too specific (a single Element), you've zoomed past Type.

## Optionlock vs Timelock (Story Limit)

### Optionlock

Story ends because options run out. Characters work through possibilities; when the last one fails (or succeeds), the story is over.

Indicators:
- A finite set of suspects, candidates, locations, attempts.
- "We've tried everything." Now what.
- Common in mysteries, heist plots, exhaustive searches.

### Timelock

Story ends because time runs out. A clock is ticking; when it hits zero, the outcome is locked.

Indicators:
- A countdown, deadline, scheduled event, expiration.
- "We have until midnight."
- Common in thrillers, ticking-bomb plots, beat-the-deadline.

### The test

When you imagine the story's end, what makes the climax *climactic*? If it's "this was the last possibility" → Optionlock. If it's "we ran out of time" → Timelock.

### Anti-indicator

"Both — they're running out of time *and* options." That can happen as flavor, but at the structural level one of them is the limit. Pick the one whose *failure* would actually end the story.

## Outcome × Judgment combinations

These are independent. Pick each on its own, then read off the ending type.

| | Outcome: Success | Outcome: Failure |
|---|---|---|
| **Judgment: Good** | Triumph | Personal Triumph |
| **Judgment: Bad** | Personal Tragedy | Tragedy |

### Outcome decision

Does the OS Goal get achieved? Note that this is *narrowly* about the Goal — not about whether good things happen overall.

### Judgment decision

Does the MC end in a satisfied internal state — at peace with themselves, the personal arc resolved well? Note that this is independent of whether the OS goal was achieved. (Hence Personal Triumph: the world's problem isn't solved, but the MC is okay. Hence Personal Tragedy: the world's problem is solved, but the MC paid for it personally.)

### The test for separating them

Imagine the final scene. Two questions:
1. Has the Goal been achieved? (Yes = Success / No = Failure)
2. Is the MC at peace internally? (Yes = Good / No = Bad)

If you keep collapsing them into "happy ending vs sad ending", you're not yet using both dynamics — that's a flatter story than Dramatica is built to model.
