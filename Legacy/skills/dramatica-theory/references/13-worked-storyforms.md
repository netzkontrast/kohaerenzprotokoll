# Worked Storyforms

> Anchor examples. Compact storyform cards for stories the source book analyzes. Use them as reference points: when you're working on your own storyform and a decision feels uncertain, compare against a known case at the same decision point. Each card distinguishes verified-from-source values from inferred values; treat inferred values as informed guesses, not authoritative.

---

## Star Wars (A New Hope, 1977)

Source: Phillips & Huntley discuss this storyform across multiple chapters; values below are pulled from their explicit treatment unless flagged as inferred.

### Throughlines

| Throughline | Player | Class |
|---|---|---|
| **Overall Story** | The war between Empire and Rebellion | **Physics** (Activity) — verified |
| **Main Character** | Luke Skywalker | **Universe** (Situation) — verified |
| **Impact Character** | Obi-Wan Kenobi | **Mind** (Fixed Attitude) — verified (book references Obi-Wan as Fixed Attitude IC) |
| **Subjective Story** | Luke ↔ Obi-Wan as student/teacher | **Psychology** — inferred (constrained by H3: pair partner of Physics) |

### Archetypes (verified, all eight present and pure)

- **Protagonist** — Luke (Pursue / Consideration)
- **Antagonist** — The Empire / Tarkin (Prevent / Reconsider)
- **Guardian** — Obi-Wan (Help / Conscience)
- **Contagonist** — Vader (Hinder / Temptation)
- **Reason** — C-3PO — inferred from book's treatment of complete archetypes
- **Emotion** — Chewbacca — inferred
- **Sidekick** — R2-D2 / Han Solo — book mentions but doesn't fix one
- **Skeptic** — Han Solo (also can carry Sidekick functions)

### Character Dynamics

| Dynamic | Value | Status |
|---|---|---|
| MC Resolve | **Change** | verified — Luke ends transformed |
| MC Growth | Start | inferred — Luke grows *into* faith/skill he initially lacks |
| MC Approach | Do-er | inferred — Luke acts on the world rather than adapts to it |
| MC Mental Sex | Linear | inferred — Luke reasons sequentially through training and battle |

### Plot Dynamics

| Dynamic | Value | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Story Driver | **Action** | inferred from book's framing of inciting events |
| Story Limit | Timelock | inferred — Death Star is approaching the Rebel base, clock pressure drives climax |
| Story Outcome | **Success** | verified — Goal is achieved |
| Story Judgment | Good | inferred — Luke ends at peace internally |

**Ending type:** Triumph (Success/Good).

### OS Thematic Story Points (verified from book)

- **Concern (Type):** **Doing** (engaging in activity — building Death Star, smuggling, transporting plans, attacking)
- **Issue (Variation):** **Skill** (everyone is compared by skills — pilots, fighters, leaders)
- **Problem (Element):** **Test** (the Empire tests Death Star; the Rebellion tests its info; trust-vs-test drives the conflict)

### What this storyform is doing

A Change MC (Luke) in a Universe situation (orphan farm boy stuck on Tatooine) confronts a Mind IC (Obi-Wan, who carries unshakeable faith in the Force) within a Physics OS (war between forces). The MC's Universe trajectory — from stuck farmer to Jedi-in-motion — mirrors the OS Goal of Doing (active opposition to the Death Star). The Crucial Element pivot is Luke choosing faith (the Force) over technology (the targeting computer) at the climax — this is his Change.

## To Kill A Mockingbird (Lee, 1960 / film 1962)

Source: discussed across the source book, particularly in the section on splitting Protagonist from MC and on the Crucial Element.

### Throughlines

| Throughline | Player | Class |
|---|---|---|
| **Overall Story** | The town of Maycomb during the Tom Robinson trial | **Mind** (Fixed Attitude) — verified |
| **Main Character** | Scout | **Universe** — inferred (constrained by H3: pair partner of Mind goes to OS-SS axis, MC-IC takes Physics-Psychology axis; Scout's struggle reads as situational — her childhood circumstance) |
| **Impact Character** | Boo Radley | **Psychology** — inferred (Boo's fixed-mind reclusiveness viewed objectively from outside) — *uncertain*, could equally read as Universe (his confinement) |
| **Subjective Story** | Scout ↔ Boo (the relationship as its own story) | inferred opposite of MC's Class |

> Honesty note: The MC and IC Classes for Mockingbird are not as clearly fixed in the source as Star Wars's are. Treat with caution. If using this card as a reference, focus on the verified items below (Resolve, Crucial Element) which are explicit in the book.

### Roles (verified)

- **Protagonist** — Atticus (the OS-throughline character pursuing the legal-defense Goal)
- **Main Character** — Scout (we live inside her)
- **Impact Character** — Boo Radley
- **Note:** This is the canonical example of MC ≠ Protagonist in the source.

### Character Dynamics

| Dynamic | Value | Status |
|---|---|---|
| MC Resolve | **Change** | verified — book explicitly: "Scout (Change)" |
| MC Growth | Stop | inferred — she grows *out* of her prejudice/judgment-from-ignorance |
| MC Approach | Be-er | inferred — she adapts (her perception, her empathy) more than she acts |
| MC Mental Sex | Holistic | inferred — Scout's mature realization is relational ("standing in his shoes") rather than chained |

### Crucial Element

**INEQUITY** — verified directly from source ("To Kill A Mockingbird Crucial Element is INEQUITY"). Since MC Resolve = Change, Inequity is the *problem* Scout gives up: her prejudice against Boo (the Inequity in her own perception) is what she abandons in the climax when she walks Boo home and sees the world from his porch.

### What this storyform is doing

A Change MC (Scout) in a personal Universe-situation (childhood, Maycomb in the Depression) confronts an IC (Boo) whose existence — invisible, mythologized, then revealed — challenges her assumed knowledge of people. The OS is Mind (the town's fixed attitudes around the trial), and Scout's personal arc parallels but is distinct from Atticus's Protagonist arc. The Crucial Element of Inequity sits at the intersection: it's both the OS's deepest problem (racial inequity in Maycomb) and Scout's personal problem (her own prejudicial inequity toward Boo). When Scout abandons Inequity at the end, the personal arc resolves — even though the OS arc is more complicated (Tom Robinson is convicted; the OS Outcome is mixed).

This is also the canonical example of why MC ≠ Protagonist matters: Atticus pursues the Goal, but Scout's inner change is the story's emotional center.

## How to use these cards

When your own storyform decision feels uncertain, find the same decision-point on a card and compare. *"My MC Resolve feels Change. Star Wars's Luke is Change. Mockingbird's Scout is Change. Both pivot on giving up a core position. Does mine?"*

When two of your decisions seem to disagree, find a card where the same two decisions coexist and check whether they cohere there. *"My MC is Change with a Crucial Element of Avoidance. Mockingbird is Change with Crucial Element Inequity. Star Wars is Change. So Change MCs give up the Element — verify mine works that way."*

These cards are not templates — copying a known storyform onto a different story produces incoherence. Use them as anchors for *individual* decisions, not as wholesale models.

## What's not here

- **Jaws** is partially treated in the source book (mainly characters, less storyform). I have not built a card because too much of it would be inferred.
- **The Wizard of Oz** appears in the source book primarily as a character-comparison case to Star Wars, not as a fully-laid-out storyform. Same reason.
- **Other films / novels** the user works on are open territory — build cards as the project develops, but mark inferred values clearly.
